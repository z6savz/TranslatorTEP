Intersections: Mysticism, Machines, and Metaphysics

August 22, 2025

The divide between logic and lyric, number and myth, is not as absolute as it seems. We follow the tangled thread between mysticism and mechanism, between symbolic thought and spiritual depth, and watch it wind through the minds of poets, mathematicians, and psychologists alike. Here, computation becomes poetry, and the soul of the machine flickers into view.


The visionary Ada Lovelace, famously saw in Charles Babbage’s Analytical Engine a new kind of symbol processor, capable, she imagined, of composing music or weaving patterns if only the laws behind those arts were formalized. Her “poetic science” was a mode of thinking that embraced abstraction as aesthetic intuition. She didn’t just ask what machines could do, but what they could represent, planting the first seeds of algorithmic creativity and artificial imagination.


This question; can machines imagine? takes on a new shape in the work of Alan Turing. With his theoretical Turing machine, he distilled the core of computation into a system of symbols, tape, and rules. Yet in his later writings, especially on artificial intelligence and the mind-body problem, Turing wrestled with philosophical riddles of thought and consciousness. If mechanical operations can emulate intelligence, can they grasp feeling? Know selfhood? Between the mechanized mind and the unknowable self, Turing opened a paradox that still haunts modern AI: we can simulate thought, but can we understand thinking?


Enter William Blake, whose 18th-century resistance to “single vision and Newton’s sleep” now reads as a prophetic protest against empirical reductionism. Blake saw mechanistic science as a narrowing of human perception, one that blinded society to imagination, divinity, and moral complexity. His illustrations of Newton hunched over a compass and of Urizen writing laws on stone weren’t rejections of knowledge, but defenses of vision. For Blake, truth came from the unveiling of the inner world through myth and symbol.


And yet the inner world became a subject of science itself in the hands of Theodore Flournoy, a psychologist who chronicled the trance narratives of a spiritualist medium. Rather than dismissing these visions, he treated them as psychic creations, spontaneous myth-making by the subconscious mind. Flournoy’s work revealed that imagination does not need intent to generate complexity. In its depths, the psyche spins worlds, much like a machine governed by cryptic inner rules. From this blend of mysticism and psychology emerged new pathways into the study of dreams, narratives, and symbols that would later influence Jung and psychology. Together, these figures draw the boundaries of a new terrain, where poetry, programming, dreams, and data trace the same geometries across flesh and wire.
